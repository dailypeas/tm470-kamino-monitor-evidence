# Literature evidence map

Accessed 13 July 2026. References are formatted for Harvard Cite Them Right and should remain alphabetised in the report.

| Source and verified reference | Contribution and limitation | PROMPT evaluation | Project decision / transferability |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alizadeh, S. and Khabbazian, M. (2025) ‘Solana’s transaction network: analysis, insights, and comparison’, *EPJ Data Science*, 14, article 48. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1140/epjds/s13688-025-00561-x | Solana-specific transaction-network evidence. Study interval and network-level analysis do not validate wallet alert thresholds. | Peer-reviewed, current and directly relevant; methods/provenance are visible. | Supports treating Solana as structurally distinct and avoiding direct transfer of Bitcoin/Ethereum assumptions. |
| Bolton, R.J. and Hand, D.J. (2002) ‘Statistical fraud detection: a review’, *Statistical Science*, 17(3), pp. 235–255. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1214/ss/1042727940 | Explains behavioural comparison and adaptive fraud-detection challenges. It predates blockchain and does not publish applicable Kamino thresholds. | Peer-reviewed methodological review with strong provenance; old but conceptually relevant. | Supports historical baselines and cautious use of deviation; transfer is conceptual only. |
| Chandola, V., Banerjee, A. and Kumar, V. (2009) ‘Anomaly detection: a survey’, *ACM Computing Surveys*, 41(3), article 15. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1145/1541880.1541882 | Defines anomalies relative to expected behaviour and surveys approaches/limitations. Not blockchain-specific. | Peer-reviewed synthesis, transparent scope and established provenance; age affects examples more than core concepts. | Supports explicit baselines, contextual interpretation and no equation of anomaly with maliciousness. |
| Hassan, M.U., Rehmani, M.H. and Chen, J. (2023) ‘Anomaly detection in blockchain networks: a comprehensive survey’, *IEEE Communications Surveys & Tutorials*, 25(1), pp. 289–318. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1109/COMST.2022.3205643 | Synthesises blockchain anomaly methods and feature categories. Broad coverage does not provide Kamino/Solana operating thresholds. | Peer-reviewed, blockchain-specific survey with clear provenance; breadth limits protocol specificity. | Supports transaction/temporal features and the decision to keep transfer to Solana cautious. |
| Kamino Finance (2026) *KLend – Kamino Lending Program*. Available at: https://github.com/Kamino-Finance/klend (Accessed: 13 July 2026). | Primary deployment source for the Lending programme ID. Not independent research. | Current implementation provenance and direct relevance; organisational source with potential self-interest. | Supplies the exact mainnet allowlist identifier; no security claim is inferred. |
| Scarfone, K. and Mell, P. (2007) *Guide to Intrusion Detection and Prevention Systems (IDPS)*. NIST SP 800-94. Available at: https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.SP.800-94 | Authoritative monitoring/detection principles, tuning and false-alert context. Enterprise IDPS rather than blockchain. | Government technical guidance with strong provenance; dated and domain-limited. | Supports observable-event monitoring, transparent alert triage and tuning; transfer is conceptual. |
| Solana (2026) *Solana RPC JSON structures*. Available at: https://solana.com/docs/rpc/json-structures (Accessed: 13 July 2026). | Defines raw/parsed transaction, account-key and instruction structures. Official but not independent evaluation. | Current primary technical documentation; high implementation relevance. | Informs normalisation and programme-ID resolution. |
| Helius (2026) *TypeScript SDK API reference*. Available at: https://www.helius.dev/docs/agents/typescript-sdk/api-reference (Accessed: 13 July 2026). | Documents standard RPC and address history capabilities. Vendor documentation and subject to change. | Current primary implementation source; commercial provenance requires cross-checking. | Informs optional retrieval; fixtures prevent vendor/API availability becoming a test dependency. |
| Zheng, X., Wan, Z., Lo, D., Xie, D. and Yang, X. (2025) ‘Why does my transaction fail? A first look at failed transactions on the Solana blockchain’, *Proceedings of ISSTA 2025*. Preprint available at: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.18055 | Large-scale analysis shows failed Solana transactions have varied causes and temporal/program patterns. It does not make repeated failure proof of hostile intent. | Recent empirical Solana research with described large dataset; preprint access and different research objective limit direct transfer. | Supports failures as an observable feature and, crucially, cautious false-positive interpretation. |
